Digital Humanities Day

March 14 Hybrid workshop: in person or zoom

The Library and Sydney Informatics Hub are hosting a free Digital Humanities day for researchers, HDR students and honours students on 14 March 2023. This will be a hybrid event that happens both online and at Fisher Library.  
The morning session will include researcher lightning talks that give insight into the current DH research being undertaken at USyd. There will also be presentations outlining some of the tools and support available in the DH space for USyd researchers and research students. The morning session will be offered both online and in-person. In-person attendees are invited to stay for a catered lunch after the presentations wrap up. 
The Sydney Informatics Hub and the Sydney Corpus Lab will deliver several workshops on digital humanities tools, including the Australian Text Analytics Platform and corpus linguistics tools. Each session will vary in how it is offered (hybrid, online or in-person), length of time and who can sign up. The workshop program and registration page will be released soon in Feburary.

SIH staff excel at SHINE! Awards

SIH staff won two awards at the 2022 Research Portfolio SHINE! Awards in December:
Data scientist Dr Sebastian Haan won the Innovation Award, which recognises outstanding innovation to solve a research problem and create new research opportunities. Sebastian collaborated with the Sydney Institute of Agriculture and the Grains Research and Development Corporation (GRDC) to develop a machine learning app to transform sparse soil measurements into fine-scale 3D predictions and depth maps of agronomically important, plant growth-limiting soil constraints to improve sustainable soil management and crop yield.   
Chris Howden and Dr Kathrin Schemann from the Statistical Consulting team won the Collaboration Award for the ‘Equity student’ project with colleagues from the Educational Innovation team. The collaboration provided an evidence-based support for elements of the new university strategy and resulted in a series of actionable, data-informed initiatives for improving the learning experience, student support and outcomes for students who experienced an educational disadvantage before coming to university.

Survey Design & Analysis Day 

March 8 Hybrid workshops: in-person or zoom 

Thanks to university-supported online platforms like Redcap and Qualtrics, delivering surveys to research participants is easier than ever. Asking the right questions within your surveys can be more of a challenge. Is it easy for your participants to understand what you are asking? Will analysis of the responses help you answer your research questions? Is your survey well designed? 
On March 8, Sydney Informatics Hub presents a day of workshops relevant to researchers who want to upskill in survey techniques. Each workshop is 90 minutes long and provides practical advice on methods used frequently in survey design and analysis. Surveys I covers design and analysis considerations common to most surveys. Surveys II delves into more advanced topics including assessing reliability and creating new indices using multiple questions.  
Register now for one or both surveys workshops to ensure that your next survey will yield valuable information for your research. 

ATAP workshop series – A hands-on guide to Semantic Tagger for your text data analysis 

March 22 Online workshop

Better Understand Your Texts by Adding Semantic Tags 
The Australian Text Analytics Platform (ATAP) project is a project that aims to provide researchers with the tools and training for analysing, processing, and exploring text. As part of this project, we have adapted with permission, a Semantic Tagger, developed by the University Centre for Computer Corpus Research on Language (UCREL) at Lancaster University. This tool uses the Python Multilingual UCREL Semantic Analysis System (PyMUSAS) to tag your text data so that you can extract token level semantic tags from your text. In addition to the USAS tags, this tool can also recognize Multi Word Expressions (MWE), i.e., expressions formed by two or more words that behave like a unit such as 'South Australia', and identifies lemmas and Part-of-Speech (POS) tags in the text. For example, in the sentence ‘President Joe Biden attended two meetings today’, the tool will tag each token with its semantic tag like this -> ‘President Joe Biden’: MWE of [Personal names], ‘attended’: [Participating], ‘two’: [Number], ‘meetings’: [Participating] and ‘today’: [Time: Present; simultaneous].
This tool is available in both English and multi-lingual (Chinese, Italian and Spanish) versions and supports saving the results locally for further analysis, enabling you to gain meaningful insights into your research questions. 
This workshop will be of interest to Researchers and HDR Students in Linguistics, Business, Law, Science, or those who are interested in working with text datasets. No background knowledge of NLP or coding experience is required for this 1.5 hour online workshop, to be delivered via Zoom. You can BYO a small dataset (10-15 texts, stored as one text per file or all texts compressed in a zip file) – or use one of our sample datasets to get started.

Demystifying statistical modelling 

February 22 Hybrid workshops: in-person or zoom 

Do you get confused about the differences between linear and logistic regression or ANOVA, ANCOVA, repeated measures and mixed models? On February 22 we will deliver three workshops from our modular statistical training system to demystify these terms and show how all of these align in the one Linear Models framework. Linear Models Module 1 explains how the common analyses of regression, ANOVA, ANCOVA and repeated measures can all be done using the same Linear Models workflow. Module 2 then extends this to the Generalised Linear Model (GLM) framework for non-normal distributions such as Logistic and Count/Poisson regression. Aligning all these different methods into the same box with the same generic workflow makes it much easier to understand and remember how to apply them, while the practical examples provided give clear instructions for later use. The final module on the day, Statistical Model Building, explores the issues around more complex statistical modelling to build multivariable models with many predictors.
