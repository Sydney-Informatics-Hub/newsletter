Keynote Speaker Announced for 2023 Core Research Facilities Symposium

9 November: in-person

The full program for the 2023 Core Research Facilities Symposium is now available. The keynote session will feature Associate Professor Arnold Lining Ju and team members Dr Zihao Wang and Fengtao Jiang speaking alongside staff engineers Jacky He and Ethel Ilagan from the Research and Prototype Foundry (RPF) facility.
Associate Professor Ju is a pioneering figure in biomedical engineering, and his interdisciplinary project to develop a microfluidic device for detecting cardiovascular disease is a well-known success story within the University and beyond. The keynote will shed light on his collaboration with the Research and Prototype Foundry (RPF), sharing an untold part of the research journey and unveiling the behind-the-scenes efforts that drive success. The session complements other elements of the program designed to showcase capabilities and help researchers to discover and leverage the facilities.  
Registration is free, including refreshments.
When: 9 November, 8:45am - 2:00pm
Where: Messel Theatre – Sydney Nanoscience Hub

Survival Analysis

10 October Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom 

Across the University of Sydney researchers in different disciplines are studying the time elapsed up to when a specified event occurs. For example, you might be interested in factors that influence survival of a person after a disease diagnosis or survival of a company after a stock market crash. Perhaps you like to study the survival time of marriage, or you are interested in how long a device or manufacturing component lasts before it fails or becomes obsolete. Survival analysis has also been applied to study staff churn or promotion, and the optimum time for soccer player substitution during a game.
To learn more about how ‘time-to event’/ survival analysis works, and how you can incorporate it into your research projects, sign up to the free Sydney Informatics Hub ‘Introduction to Survival Analysis’ workshop happening on the 10th of October.

Experimental Design 

24 October Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom 

Are you planning to conduct quantitative research? If so, you will need to give careful thought to the design of your study. Regardless of whether you will be undertaking an observational study, a randomised control trial, or a study of some other type, a well-thought-out study design is crucial to ensure that your results can be readily interpreted and published.
Designing a study involves making numerous choices, which can sometimes make it challenging to know where to begin. We plot a path through these important choices with our experimental design workflow, emphasising the connection between how you plan to analyse your experiment and how you design your experiment. Throughout the workshop, we present key design choices applicable to widely-used study types and place them in the context of mitigating bias and error. We also emphasise how the design process is often iterative, in that improvements on an initial design can greatly strengthen your conclusions.
We outline well-known design challenges of study design, such as sampling to ensure the representativeness of your study results, or calculating an adequate sample size to ensure sufficient statistical power (covered in more detail in our Power and Sample Size calculation workshop). We also discuss design choices specific to designed experiments, such as randomisation and blocking. Even if you are not planning to conduct a designed experiment, understanding these design choices can be helpful. It can help you to understand how aspects of randomisation can be mimicked in an observational study or simply to assess the quality of published experimental research relevant in your field. 
Participants are encouraged to bring their own experimental design questions to the workshop. By attending this workshop, we encourage researchers to develop an optimal experimental design to address their research question. Whether you are a new or experienced researcher you can attend this workshop to build and improve your experimental design skill.
Come along on 24th October to find out more!
