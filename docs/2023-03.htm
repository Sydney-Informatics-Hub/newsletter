Project spotlight:

Identifying biomarkers in COVID-19 patients around the world 

Associate Professor Benjamin Tang and the PREDICT-19 Consortium have been working with us to identify novel biomarkers associated with disease progression in COVID-19 patients. The PREDICT-19 Consortium has been examining the broad range of symptoms and clinical outcomes of COVID-19 patients and the role our immune systems play in infection. Working with patient data from across Australia, Europe, Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and North America, the group has identified genetic signatures and immune pathways associated with disease severity and potential prognostic biomarkers that may supplement conventional diagnostic tools in future viral pandemics.  
SIH bioinformaticians, Dr Tracy Chew and Dr Nandan Deshpande were a part of an international team of bioinformaticians that processed and analysed over 900 samples. SIH played a critical role in building scalable bioinformatics workflows for the PREDICT-19 Consortium, acquiring the high volume of computing resources needed to process the sequence data, and addressing specific research questions about risk stratification in patients.

Project spotlight:

The ASPIRE trial

A Systems aPproach to enhancing community-based medIcation REview (ASPIRE trial) is a MRFF-funded trial led by Professor Timothy Chen, School of Pharmacy, to investigate whether a Primary Health Network (PHN)-based model for improving timely post-discharge medication reviews reduces 90-day unplanned hospital readmissions. 
The trial has a cross-sectional stepped wedge randomised cluster (SW-CRT) design. SW-CRT is a pragmatic trial design that allows researchers to implement a real-world intervention while also assessing its impact. In a stepped wedge trial data are collected pre- and post- intervention for all clusters. Rather than randomising each trial centre (cluster) to a different intervention, each centre is randomised to a different sequence (starting time for the intervention). One trial centre was allocated to each sequence, with each centre consisting of a hospital and co-located PHN. As part of the ASPIRE trial design, all hospitals were required to employ a research pharmacist before the planned start of the trial. This proved possible at three sites, but not at the fourth. As the trial involves a data linkage phase and a hard end date, delaying the start date was not an option.  
If randomisation went ahead as planned and the delayed centre was allocated to the first sequence, this would have presented an unfeasible logistical challenge for roll out of the intervention at this centre. Faced with a difficult decision, Professor Chen and the research team contacted the Statistical Consulting Unit for independent advice on randomisation of the clusters.    
Our question was time-sensitive and so we really appreciated the responsiveness of the service. We knew statisticians had differing opinions on the benefits of randomising a small number of clusters, which was part of the reason for seeking independent advice. We outlined the issue and submitted it through the SIH website. Within a week, we met with Alex Shaw and Alexandra Green to discuss the issue. Alex provided a ‘Goldilocks’ solution of conducting a constrained randomisation. We felt limiting the possible sequences for the delayed centre was a pragmatic solution to the problem. Alex also conducted the randomisation for us. It is great to know that we have this service available for researchers and statisticians alike and I would highly recommend this service to others.
For statistical consulting on your experimental design or analysis question, contact Sydney Informatics Hub via our website.

ATAP workshop series – A hands-on guide to Semantic Tagger for your text data analysis 

March 22 Online workshop

Better Understand Your Texts by Adding Semantic Tags 
The Australian Text Analytics Platform (ATAP) project is a project that aims to provide researchers with the tools and training for analysing, processing, and exploring text. As part of this project, we have adapted with permission, a Semantic Tagger, developed by the University Centre for Computer Corpus Research on Language (UCREL) at Lancaster University. This tool uses the Python Multilingual UCREL Semantic Analysis System (PyMUSAS) to tag your text data so that you can extract token level semantic tags from your text. In addition to the USAS tags, this tool can also recognize Multi Word Expressions (MWE), i.e., expressions formed by two or more words that behave like a unit such as 'South Australia', and identifies lemmas and Part-of-Speech (POS) tags in the text. For example, in the sentence ‘President Joe Biden attended two meetings today’, the tool will tag each token with its semantic tag like this -> ‘President Joe Biden’: MWE of [Personal names], ‘attended’: [Participating], ‘two’: [Number], ‘meetings’: [Participating] and ‘today’: [Time: Present; simultaneous].
This tool is available in both English and multi-lingual (Chinese, Italian and Spanish) versions and supports saving the results locally for further analysis, enabling you to gain meaningful insights into your research questions. 
This workshop will be of interest to Researchers and HDR Students in Linguistics, Business, Law, Science, or those who are interested in working with text datasets. No background knowledge of NLP or coding experience is required for this 1.5 hour online workshop, to be delivered via Zoom. You can BYO a small dataset (10-15 texts, stored as one text per file or all texts compressed in a zip file) – or use one of our sample datasets to get started.
