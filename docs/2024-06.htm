SIH Data Scientist secondment opportunity  

Do you love using data to solve problems and understand the world? Sydney Informatics Hub is excited to announce that we have a secondment opportunity for a Data Scientist to join our team full-time for ~11 months (end date May 12, 2025).
As one of our Data Scientists, you'll provide data science and AI project consultancy across the entire university, collaborating with researchers in science, engineering, health, humanities, and more. Your role will be integral in driving outcomes, from project design to data extraction, modelling, visualisation, and reporting. If you're ready to translate your coding and ML/AI skills into impactful outcomes, we’d strongly encourage you to apply for this role (closes midnight Sunday 9 June, 2024).

SIH offers integrated support for projects involving high-throughput data

Biomedical research is now well and truly in the high-throughput age. Medicine and health research projects routinely incorporate collection of whole-genome, metabolome, or microbiome data, which often pose substantial data analysis challenges to researchers. Bioinformaticians and statisticians within the Sydney Informatics Hub are working more closely than ever to support a growing number of researchers in their analysis of high-throughput biological data. We work with researchers at every stage of their project, from choosing platforms and designing experiments through to processing raw data, analysis and interpretation.
Recently, a team of researchers from the University of Sydney’s Schools of Pharmacy and Veterinary Science and the University of Newcastle’s Priority Research Centre in Reproductive Science explored the relationship between the equine microbiome, pro- and pre-biotics and fertility. They approached SIH to undertake a project including processing and quality control of the microbiome data to produce analysis-ready data, followed by detailed statistical analysis. Medical researcher and clinician Dr Giselle Cooke lead two publications ("Effect of probiotics and prebiotics on the composition of the equine fecal and seminal microbiomes and sperm quality: A pilot study" and "The semen microbiome of miniature pony stallions") and this work contributed to the award of her PhD degree. 
We encourage all researchers planning work with high-throughput data to contact us before you collect any data, for a discussion about how we can help you maximise the impact of your research.

SIH is improving command-line infrastructure for Australian life  scientists 

The Sydney Informatics Hub is leading a new Australian BioCommons platforms project called BioCLI aimed at helping life scientists to tackle the challenges of working at the command-line interface (CLI). Over the next 3 years, we’ll be working with our partners at the National Computational Infrastructure (NCI), and the Pawsey Supercomputing Research Centre to establish environments and services to reduce friction for processing and analysis of molecular data at scale.
We are currently working on:  
  • Developing public preconfigured virtual machine images containing essential bioinformatics-specific tools, libraries, and datasets for national, institutional, and commercial infrastructures;  
  • Facilitating access to specialised hardware (GPUs!) required to run bioinformatics tools in an accelerated environment  
Keep up to date with the latest BioCLI project developments on the Australian BioCommons website and be sure to register for our upcoming entry-level webinar “What exactly is bioinformatics?” delivered by Dr Georgie Samaha, Product Owner for BioCLI and Bioinformatics Group Lead at SIH.  

Want to get involved? Email us at sih.info@sydney.edu.au.  


Training with real-world genetic data to advance Australia’s urgent conservation goals 

The Australian BioCommons’ Genetic Outlier Analysis workshop series is coming to Sydney and will run 4-5 July 2024 at the UNSW, Kensington Campus. It will introduce the basics of genetic selection analysis, and step through the process of identifying signals of selection using participants' own data (or an example genomic dataset) using the outlier analysis method.
The two-day workshop will be led by Dr Katarina Stuart from the University of Auckland, thanks to a grant from the Genetics Society of AustralAsia (GSA). It is fully subsidised for Australian researchers and/or members of GSA (subject to selection).

Statistical workshops to help you plan your study design and analysis

1 July Hybrid workshops: in-person or Zoom

Our Foundational Statistical Series is designed to help researchers plan their study design and analysis and thereby to set themselves up for research success.
Research Essentials gives an 8-step generic workflow from study inception to publication that can be applied to any statistical analysis, no matter the analysis method, discipline or software used. By teaching researchers to classify their data into appropriate data types and to use appropriate visualisations to explore their data this workshop empowers them to choose the appropriate analysis method and gives examples of common inferential analyses.
Experimental Design focuses on Step 2 of the 8-step generic research workflow and covers the key aspects of experimental design that researchers and students may need to apply in their research. Topics include sampling, validity, randomisation, bias, blocking and replication. The workshop provides the opportunity to discuss researcher’s own research question and associated experimental design.

'Time-to-event' Analysis - SIH Introductory Workshop

1 July Hybrid workshops: in-person or Zoom

Across different University of Sydney faculties, researchers are studying the time elapsed up to when a specified event occurs. For example, researchers study the survival of a person after a disease diagnosis or of a company after a stock market crash. Perhaps you are interested in survival time of marriage, time to promotion, how long a device lasts before it fails or becomes obsolete or the optimum time for soccer player substitution during a game.
To learn more about how ‘time-to event’/ survival analysis works, and how you can incorporate it into your research projects, sign up to the free Sydney Informatics Hub ‘Introduction to Survival Analysis’ workshop on the 1st of July.
