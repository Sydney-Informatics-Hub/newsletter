Genome Lab: The new online workbench from Galaxy Australia   

Want to do genomics without the hassle of learning to use the command-line? As a part of the Australian BioCommons activities to support Australian genomics researchers, Galaxy Australia has released its new user-friendly Genome Lab for the Australian genomics community. 
Genome Lab offers a curated collection of bioinformatics tools, workflows, and tutorials for data preparation, genome assembly, and genome annotation. Give it a go at https://genome.usegalaxy.org.au/

Experimental Design

18 July Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom 

Are you planning to conduct quantitative research? If so, you will need to give careful thought to the design of your study. Regardless of whether you will be undertaking an observational study, a randomised control trial, or a study of some other type, a well-thought-out study design is crucial to ensure that your results can be readily interpreted and published. 
Designing a study involves making numerous choices, which can sometimes make it challenging to know where to begin. In our Experimental Design workshop, we plot a path through these important choices with our experimental design workflow, emphasising the connection between how you plan to analyse your experiment and how you design your experiment. Throughout the workshop, we present key design choices applicable to widely-used study types and place them in the context of mitigating bias and error. We also emphasise how the design process is often iterative, in that improvements on an initial design can greatly strengthen your conclusions.
We outline well-known design challenges of study design, such as sampling to ensure the representativeness of your study results, or calculating an adequate sample size to ensure sufficient statistical power (covered in more detail in our Power and Sample Size calculation workshop). We also discuss design choices specific to designed experiments, such as randomisation and blocking. Even if you are not planning to conduct a designed experiment, understanding these design choices can be helpful. It can help you to understand how aspects of randomisation can be mimicked in an observational study or simply to assess the quality of published experimental research relevant in your field.
Participants are encouraged to bring their own experimental design questions to the workshop. By attending this workshop, we encourage researchers to develop an optimal experimental design to address their research question. Whether you are a new or experienced researcher you can attend this workshop to build and improve your experimental design skill. We hope to see you on July 18! Register now!

Hacky Hour

19 July, 3-4pm: Zoom

Hacky Hour is a regular meetup where researchers – students, staff, and university affiliates – can gather in a social environment to collaborate and receive research support. Experts and mentors from Sydney Informatics Hub and across the University will be available to advise and answer questions on coding, data analytics, or digital tools. Come along and bring your stats, coding, and data science problems for us to solve! 
Hacky Hour is held on the third Wednesday of every month. You can join us via Zoom.

PacBio HiFi Sequencing Showcase 

28 July: in-person

Long-read sequencing technologies like PacBio HiFi sequencing are quickly rising in popularity in genomics research. HiFi reads are being used across a range of applications including de novo assembly, variant discovery, RNA sequencing, and epigenetic characterisation. 
If you’d like to learn more and connect with other researchers using the HiFi technology, join researchers at the APAC PacBio HiFi Sequencing Experience Tour on the 28th of July. Researchers from the Australasian Wildlife Genomics Group at University of Sydney and the Garvan Institute of Medical Research will be talking about how they are applying this technology to clinical genomics and wildlife conservation.
