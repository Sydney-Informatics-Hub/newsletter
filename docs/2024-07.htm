Director's Update  

Over the past few months much of my focus has been on finalising our contract with the Australian Plant Phenomics Network which is a National Collaborative Research Infrastructure Facility (NCRIS). We are now officially a node of the APPN, since the signing of the phase 2 contract last month.  This is an expansion of the APPN to have nodes in all mainland states in Australia.  As you can imagine by the name, the network’s focus is characterisation of the form and function of plants using sensing and analytics.
Our Node Director is Dr William (Tam) Salter who is based in Narrabri, where the node will be headquartered with a range of plant sensing infrastructure to be available for researchers to access. I would like thank Tam for all of his efforts to assist with finalising the contract and I looking forward to working together for next 5 years. The Sydney Informatics Hub will provide data science support for the node with a particular focus on developing pipelines to process and analyse raw sensor data, enabling predictions of plant traits. This capability will be complemented by technical staff to maintain and operate the sensing infrastructure at Narrabri. We are also setting up processes so that all our researchers can access this mobile and transportable infrastructure.
I would also like to acknowledge the efforts of Professors Brent Kaiser (Director of the Sydney Institute of Agriculture) and Richard Trethowan (Director of the Plant Breeding Institute) who have been engaging with the APPN for a number of years to make the node a reality. 
The APPN node will be operated within SIH, complementing our long-term NCRIS engagement with BioPlatforms Australia through the BioCommons, and the Australian Research Data Commons. Together, they provide a real opportunity to build complementary platforms for acceleration of our research in many disciplines across the university. 
Some details can be found here about the new structure of APPN.
If you have any questions please contact Tam at william.salter@sydney.edu.au. 
Regards, 
Tom
Prof. Thomas Bishop, Director, Sydney Informatics Hub

SIH client recognised in King’s Birthday Honours 

Congratulations to our client Professor Helen Reddel, who has been awarded a Member of the Order of Australia for significant service to respiratory medicine, and to medical research.
Professor Reddel has dedicated her career to understanding and improving outcomes for patients with asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). She is the Chair of the Global Initiative for Asthma (GINA) Science Committee, which leads the way in ensuring novel research discoveries are translated into best practice guidelines internationally. Sydney Informatics Hub has been working with Professor Reddel to analyse asthma inhaler usage patterns as part of a collaboration involving the Woolcock Institute of Medical Research and AstraZeneca.

Project spotlight: seekRNA delivers new pathway for gene editing 

Congratulations to Dr Sandro Ataide and Dr Rezwan Siddiquee who have developed a novel gene editing tool that has greater accuracy and flexibility than CRISPR! Their findings have been published in Nature Communications. You can read more about their work on the University’s news site.
SIH bioinformatician, Dr Cali Willet, created a computational workflow to identify conserved DNA sequence motifs enabling site-specific insertion of cargo DNA into the genome. Insertion of cargo DNA in a site-specific manner is fundamental to safe genome engineering. The study focused on two insertion sequence families that the Ataide lab had identified as having unique capabilities for precise and efficient genome editing, which they have dubbed ‘seekRNA’. The public code repository can be easily reimplemented on any family of insertion sequences, opening the door to seek more exciting discoveries from this fascinating class of transposable genetic element!

Meta-Analysis: an introduction

22 July Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom

Are you currently working on a systematic review or plan to do so in the future? If yes, then it is important for you to know about meta-analysis, a statistical technique that can add a valuable quantitative component to your qualitative literature review. In contrast to primary studies that combine information across individual subjects, meta-analysis combines information across multiple studies.
This powerful technique can provide a more precise estimate of the quantitative effect of a particular phenomenon, whether it be an educational intervention, drug treatment, disease incidence in the human population, or anything else. In addition to increased accuracy, meta-analysis provides a quantitative snapshot of the existing research in your field, enabling you to compare the contribution of individual studies to an overall estimate. You can use the technique to better understand conflicting results across multiple studies by examining potential publication bias, and the effect of different population or study subgroups.
To learn more about how meta-analysis works, and how you can incorporate it into your research projects, sign up to the free Sydney Informatics Hub workshop happening on the 22 of July.

Building Interpretable Models to tell your research story: Linear Models 3

22 July Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom

Statistical analysis is more than just building the best predictive model, it should also enable researchers to make the discoveries required to build new knowledge. Constructing engaging narratives about their research is also invaluable as researchers look to connect with their field, the community and funding bodies. To do this researchers need to test hypotheses, uncover unknown patterns, and present results in insightful, intuitive and memorable ways. In short, researchers need to build interpretable models.
Our Linear Models 3 workshop to be delivered on 22 July explores tips and tricks to make your statistical analyses do just that.
Topics covered will be:
  • Reporting tricks that aid interpretation - estimated marginal means, confidence vs prediction intervals, applying and correcting for multiple comparisons, reporting variable ‘importance’, plus other reporting and interpretation tricks;
  • Model Parameterisation using the Design Matrix - interpreting categorical predictor parameters, dummy and effects coding;
  • More on Mixed Models - introducing the random slope.

Multivariate: Dimension Reduction

22 July Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom

If you printed out all the data you have collected for your quantitative research study, how many pages would it require? Could you carry it in a briefcase, or would you need a large truck? Increasingly, researchers are working with large datasets, but not all large datasets are alike. Some are ‘tall’ (they contain relatively few pieces of information on a lot of subjects), others are ‘wide’ (containing a lot of information on relatively few subjects). If your dataset falls into the ‘wide’ group, you are likely dealing with a high-dimensional dataset. These kinds of datasets are increasingly common in medicine and health (e.g. high-throughput biology data) and have been collected for many years in other fields (e.g. species abundance in ecology or psychometric questionnaires in psychology).
High dimensional datasets can be tricky to analyse. Trying to explore them by just looking at your raw data is like being inside a labyrinth. What you see in front of you won’t give you the insight you need into the bigger picture. Enter dimension reduction, which allows you to reduce the complexity of your dataset while retaining the overall patterns that yield insight into your study population. Dimension reduction sounds complicated but applying it to your research doesn’t have to be. In our dimension reduction workshop, we use simple, real-world examples to illustrate the key concepts involved, and avoid getting bogged down in mathematical detail.
Maybe like many others you have heard of techniques like PCA and nMDS, but aren’t sure what they are or how to use them. We step you through applying these dimension reduction techniques from start to finish: from raw data to full interpretation of the method outputs. The result is an accessible introduction to dimension reduction techniques, and a launching pad to explore the applications of these methods in your own research field.
Cut through the complexity and reveal the hidden patterns in your data by signing up for our Multivariate Statistical Methods I: Dimension Reduction workshop on 22 July.
