Bioinformatics is a vital component of biological and medical research, supporting data-driven discoveries, genomic medicine, and the development of innovative therapeutics. As the life sciences become increasingly data-driven, ‘doing’ bioinformatics to extract, process, analyse, and interpret this data has become an essential skill for every molecular life scientist. Depending on your unique applications and the biological questions at hand, the sorts of methods you will apply to bring your data to life will vary dramatically and knowing where to start can feel impossible. 
In this Masterclass, we will delve into the vast world of bioinformatics, illuminating key concepts and experimental design considerations. High Performance Computing at Sydney   

26 September: in-person workshops

Are you frustrated with the length of time it takes to run your code? Learn how to utilise greater resources to speed up your time to science on the 26th of September with our dedicated in-person workshops, Introduction to Artemis HPC and the complementary Data transfer and Research Data Storage (RDS) for HPC. 
As data continues to be collected both in size and quantity, processing large amounts of data, and knowing which tools researchers should use is a common barrier. Yet you may be surprised how accessible computing solutions are to solve these barriers - no need for a degree in IT to solve your research question. In these workshops you will learn about the University of Sydney’s high-performance computer (HPC) ‘Artemis’, including directory structure, software, and how to submit and monitor compute jobs using the PBS Pro scheduling software. The workshops are applicable to using national HPC facilities and cloud services available at Sydney. Artemis is available at no cost to University of Sydney staff and students. 
Learning using high-performance computing offers several advantages: 
1. Speed and Efficiency: HPC systems are designed to deliver immense computational power, enabling faster and more efficient execution of complex learning algorithms. This allows researchers to process and analyse large datasets more quickly, reducing the time required to obtain results. 
2. Big Data Analytics: With the exponential growth of data, HPC enables efficient processing and analysis of vast amounts of data. Machine learning algorithms often require large datasets for training, and HPC can significantly speed up these processes, leading to improved accuracy and insights.
3. Scalability: HPC systems offer high scalability, allowing users to leverage distributed computing resources and parallel processing capabilities. This scalability enables the handling of massive datasets, complex models, and computationally intensive tasks.
4. Enable Domain Specific Software Use: HPC plays a crucial role in various scientific domains, such as genomics, climate modelling, physics, chemical engineering and astronomy. Specialised software in these domains exist that are designed to be run on HPC's.

Survey Design & Analysis day 

12 September Hybrid workshops: in-person or Zoom 

Thanks to university-supported online platforms like Redcap and Qualtrics, delivering surveys to research participants is easier than ever. Asking the right questions within your surveys can be more of a challenge. Is it easy for your participants to understand what you are asking? Will analysis of the responses help you answer your research questions? Is your survey well designed? On September 12, Sydney Informatics Hub will present a day of workshops relevant to researchers who want to upskill in survey techniques. Each workshop is 90 minutes long and provides practical advice on methods used frequently in survey design and analysis. Surveys I covers design and analysis considerations common to most surveys. Surveys II delves into more advanced topics including assessing reliability and creating new indices using multiple questions.
Register now for one or both surveys workshops to ensure that your next survey will yield valuable information for your research.

Meta-analysis 

25 September Hybrid workshop: in-person or Zoom 

Are you currently working on a systematic review or plan to do so in the future? If yes, then it is important for you to know about meta-analysis, a statistical technique that can add a valuable quantitative component to your qualitative literature review. In contrast to primary studies that combine information across individual subjects, meta-analysis combines information across multiple studies. This powerful technique can provide a more precise estimate of the quantitative effect of a particular phenomenon, whether it be an educational intervention, drug treatment, disease incidence in the human population, or anything else. 
In addition to increased accuracy, meta-analysis provides a quantitative snapshot of the existing research in your field, enabling you to compare the contribution of individual studies to an overall estimate. You can use the technique to better understand conflicting results across multiple studies by examining potential publication bias, and the effect of different population or study subgroups.
To learn more about how meta-analysis works, and how you can incorporate it into your research projects, sign up to the free Sydney Informatics Hub meta-analysis workshop happening on the 25th of September. Register now!

BioCommons workshop: RNASeq: reads to differential genes and pathways  

September & October: Introductory RNA sequencing training 

Are you a biologist or clinician interested in applying gene expression analysis to your research? RNA sequencing (RNAseq) is a popular technique for unravelling the intricacies of gene activity. RNAseq experiments have many different research applications, and it can be challenging to appreciate all the design considerations, processing requirements, and limitations of RNAseq experiments as they apply to you. Join SIH bioinformaticians to equip yourself with this essential knowledge in our upcoming RNAseq training series. We’ll be covering experimental design, data processing methods, and all the essential computational considerations. 
On September 6th, Dr Nandan Deshpande will be presenting a webinar with the Australian BioCommons. He will guide us through the end-to-end process of RNAseq experiments, starting at the lab bench with RNA extraction through to the application of computational methods for gaining biological insights. Attendance is free and open to all. See the Australian BioCommons events page for registration details. 
If you’re looking for more hands-on experience doing RNAseq analysis on the command-line, join Dr Nandan Deshpande and Dr Georgie Samaha for a workshop on October 11-12th. This workshop is a part of the Australian BioCommons national training programme and participation is subject to application with selection. Applications close at 11:59pm AEST, Monday 25 September 2023. See the Australian BioCommons events page for registration details.
